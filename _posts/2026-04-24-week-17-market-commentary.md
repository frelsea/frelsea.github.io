Secondhand values for dry bulk vessels continued to appreciate across most size classes during week 17, with the divergence between larger and smaller tonnage remaining pronounced. Five-year-old Capesize vessels are now valued at approximately $70.5 million, up 12% year-on-year, while resale units command around $81 million. Ten-year-old Capesize tonnage has seen a steeper increase, reaching $54.5 million, a 22% gain on 2025 levels. Kamsarmax and Ultramax resales are each assessed at around $42 to $42.5 million, up approximately 11% year-on-year. The appreciation at the larger end of the dry bulk market has continued ahead of freight market signals, reflecting owner confidence in the long-term supply outlook rather than short-term earnings momentum.

Transaction volumes were solid across the week. In the Post-Panamax and Kamsarmax segment, an 88,000-dwt 2006-built vessel sold for $12.9 million to Chinese buyers, while a 76,000-dwt 2012-built unit achieved $16.5 million. A modern 64,000-dwt 2018-built Ultramax changed hands at $32 million, and two 2027-built resale units were reported at $36.5 million each to Far Eastern interests. In the Supramax segment, a 60,000-dwt 2016-built vessel sold for $26 million, with several 2011 to 2012-built Japanese units trading between $18.5 million and $20.4 million. Older 2006 to 2007-built Supramaxes transacted in the $9.8 to $10 million range. Two 2012-built VLOCs of 205,000 dwt each changed hands en bloc for in excess of $90 million to Chinese interests.

Tanker secondhand values have risen more sharply than dry bulk equivalents in percentage terms. Suezmax resales are assessed at $175 million, up 21% year-on-year, with five-year units at $120 million reflecting the same annual gain. Aframax resales stand at $88.8 million, also up 20%. MR2 resales are assessed at $53.5 million, up 7%. Activity in the tanker sector was active. Two 2026-built Suezmax resale vessels sold at $120 million each to Greek buyers, and three 2014-built Aframax units transacted en bloc at $195 million. An older 2007-built Suezmax achieved $50.1 million and a 2018-built Aframax $78 million. In the products sector, a 2026-built MR2 resale unit sold at approximately $50 million, while several older MR2s traded between $13.3 million and $25.75 million depending on age and class status. A 2006-built VLCC of 302,000 dwt was reported sold at $60 million.

Newbuilding prices were largely stable week-on-week with marginal increases across most segments. Capesize orders at Chinese yards are priced at $75 to $77 million, Kamsarmax at $37 to $38 million, Ultramax at $34 to $35 million and Handysize at $30 to $33 million. Korean-built VLCCs are quoted at $131 million, Suezmax at $89 million, Aframax at $75.5 million and MR2 at approximately $51 million. Contracting activity during the week included four plus two Capesize units ordered at an Imabari yard at $39.2 million each for 2028 to 2029 delivery, and two LR2 tankers placed at a Chinese yard at $76.5 million each for 2028 delivery. Three small LPG and ammonia carriers were ordered at approximately $53 million each for delivery in 2028 and 2029.

Demolition values were broadly stable across all destinations. Indian subcontinent rates for bulkers ranged from $440 to $480 per light displacement tonne, with tankers fetching $420 to $480 per light displacement tonne. Bangladesh offered $360 to $480 for bulkers and $390 to $510 for tankers. Pakistan and Turkey continue to offer lower rates, with Turkish bulker levels between $270 and $460 per light displacement tonne. Enquiry in the recycling market remained modest, with limited volume of tonnage reported sold for demolition during the week.
