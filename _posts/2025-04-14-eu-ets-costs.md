title: "EU ETS costs are now a line item — owners ignoring this are mispricing assets"
date: 2025-04-14
---

The EU ETS shipping regime is no longer a future concern. It is a present cost. For a 2005-built Suezmax running 60% EU-covered voyages, the annual ETS bill at current carbon prices runs to roughly $800k–$1.1M. That figure is not reflected in how most small owners are calculating their net earnings or their asset values when selling. Buyers who understand this are extracting it in price negotiations. Sellers who do not are leaving money on the table — or worse, buying themselves into a structural loss.

The mechanism is straightforward. From 2024, shipping fell under the EU ETS. Vessels above 5,000 GT calling EU ports must surrender ETS allowances (EUAs) for CO₂ emitted. In 2024, 40% of emissions were covered. In 2025, 70%. From 2026, 100%. The ramp-up is not hypothetical — it is already in the regulation.

What is less well understood is that the cost does not fall equally across vessel types, trade patterns, or technical specifications. A fuel-efficient 2012-built vessel with a well-maintained main engine and a proactive technical manager will have a materially lower ETS exposure than a poorly-maintained 2007-built unit running the same routes. That delta is now an asset value differential — not an operational footnote.

> A vessel that looks like a peer on broker valuation but carries a $600k/year ETS disadvantage is not a peer. It is a different asset class.

For owners approaching a sale, the conversation has shifted. Buyers are doing ETS sensitivity analysis before signing LOIs. They are asking for noon report data, CII ratings, and emission factors. Owners who cannot produce this data — or whose vessels score poorly — are finding it harder to support asking prices that looked reasonable twelve months ago.

The fix, for existing owners, is not complicated: invest in the data first, then in the vessel if the analysis supports it. Scrubber retrofits, engine tuning, and voyage optimisation all have quantifiable ETS payback periods. For many older assets, the numbers do not close. That is also useful information — it clarifies whether the right move is to invest or to sell before the CII trajectory becomes the story.
